Finding Trails and Hikes on gaiagps.com

You can search for Trails and Hikes by clicking on POIs (points of interest) on the main map on gaiagps.com.

What is a Hike?

Hikes are automatically added when the website finds a trail marked on the Gaia Topo map that matches multiple track recordings by users. This is how we ensure trails listed are open and hikeable.

Find Hikes

Before you start, make Gaia Topo the map source with these steps: Change, Add, and Layer Map Sources on the Main Map

  1. Click on a POI -- trailhead icon, campground, peak, etc. -- to view nearby Hikes.
  2. Hover over the name to highlight the Hike, or click on the name to view more information about the Hike.
  3. Click the "+" to save the Hike to your account.  It will show on your device the next time it syncs .
  4. To view all the Hikes in this area in a new tab, click "Known Routes."

2) This will display your Layers menu.

You can access a number of map sources for free on gaiagps.com. Preview the entire map catalog here.

To add additional sources follow these steps:

1) Click "Add Map Layers" from the Map Layers list.


2) A list of map categories will appear on the left-hand side of the screen. Click the category that suits your needs.



3) Click the + to add the source (or the - to remove it)

Layering Multiple Map Sources

A Premium Membership will allow you to layer multiple map sources and adjust their transparency.

1) Click the Layers icon.

2) This will display your Layers menu.

  • Your currently selected layers are in the "Visible Layer" section
  • Additional available map layers are listed in the "Hidden Layers" section

3) Select the green checkmark next to a hidden layer to add it to the Visible Layers section and display it on the main map. You can do this with multiple layers. Unselect the checkmark to hide the layer.

4) Use the slider under each visible layer to adjust its transparency.

5) Select the "X" at any time to close the Layers menu.

Using coordinates to search and create waypoints

You can create a waypoint with known coordinates in the following formats:

  • Decimal Degrees (lat/long): 42.15170, -122.50923
  • Degrees, minutes, seconds: 44°04'28.8"N 121°30'19.3"W
  • UTM: 10T 645965 4865969
  • MGRS: 10TFP4596565969 ( note: make sure to remove spaces when using MGRS coordinates )

After you create a waypoint, it will display on the website in decimal degrees. You can view your waypoints in your desired coordinate type in the app by following the steps at the bottom of this article.

  1. Type or paste in your coordinates into the search bar.



  2. The search results will appear in decimal degrees format. Click the search results.







  3. A temporary waypoint will appear on the map, marking the location. Click the waypoint to save.



Display waypoints in different coordinates types:

You can view different coordinate types in the app.

  • Backup/sync your account in the app ( iOS / Android )
  • Tap Settings> Units> Coordinate Type

Create and Measure Routes on gaiagps.com

This article will show you how to:

  • Create a route on the website.
  • Plan a trip along existing hiking or biking trails and roads with Snap-to-Trail modes.
  • Measure point-to-point distances and map out off-trail adventures using the Straight-Line mode.
  • Generate elevation profiles and calculate the length of your route.

Create a Route

  1. Click Explore the Map or navigate to gaiagps.com/map/
  2. Search for a place you would like to plan a route.
  3. Click the Create Route icon.







  4. Click the Routing Mode dropdown menu to change the type of route.
    - Select Hiking , Cycling , and Driving to snap to the nearest trails or roads. ( Snap-to-Tail notes )
    - Select Straight line to manually draw line-segmented routes. ( Straight-Line notes )







  5. Click the map to create the starting point for your route.
  6. Click the map again to add nodes along your route. Add as many nodes as you'd like.
    - Click, hold, and drag the map to pan around.
    - Scroll to zoom the map in and out.




  1. View route distance and cumulative ascent/descent in the menu at the bottom of your screen.



  1. Hover over the interactive elevation profile to see corresponding locations along your route.



  1. Convert any route node to a waypoint:
    - Click a node, then click the green waypoint icon.
    - Name and save your waypoint.





  1. Delete or adjust route nodes:
    - Click a node, then click the red trashcan to delete.
    - Click, hold, and drag a node to move it to a new location.





Save the Route

  1. Click Save when you are finished plotting your route.
  2. Add a Route Title and any Notes .
  3. Change the route's color with the dropdown menu.







  4. Your route is now saved. Sync your data to access the route on your devices.

Edit your Routes

To edit a route, follow the instructions here: Edit a Saved Route on gaiagps.com

Archiving, Unarchiving, and Recovering Lost Data

This article will show you how to archive and unarchive data and recover data deleted from the app.

  • If you accidentally deleted something from the app, you can recover it by unarchiving it, as long as your data was synced before you deleted it.
  • Deleting data from gaiagps.com also deletes it from your devices, and cannot be recovered.
  • Archiving data allows you to remove data from the app without permanently deleting it.
  • Archived data is removed from your device and saved to your web account.  You can then unarchive it and sync it back to your device when you need it.

Archive Data

1. From the drop down menu under your profile name, choose the data type (e.g., Tracks, Routes, Waypoints, Maps) you are looking for.

2. Archive data by toggling the sync column to "Off."  You can also mass select data to sync by using the checkboxes on the left side of the data list and then click "Toggle Sync."

Note: Toggling sync to OFF will remove the selected data from all devices synced with gaiagps.com . It will not be deleted.  It will also remove the data from the main map on gaiagps.com.


Unarchive/Restore Data

1. From the drop down menu under your profile name, choose the data type (e.g., Tracks, Routes, Waypoints, Maps) you are looking for.

2.  Click the "Show Archived" button in the upper right (see picture below).

3. Toggle sync from "Off" back to "On."  You can also mass select data to sync by using the checkboxes on the left side of the data list and then click "Toggle Sync."

"Unsupported file type" with a .gpx? - Resolving .gpx.xml issues on Mac

We've encountered this reoccurring issue on Apple devices sometimes. It seems Macs don't always recognize .gpx file extensions as being .xml files, and thus add an extra extension.

You can fix this by modifying the file extension.

In Finder, locate the file. Right-click and select "get info".

​

Managing Your Data

Import a Custom Map Source

Many map sources you use in other websites or programs can be imported into Gaia GPS, because they are "TMS" map sources. TMS map sources break maps into square tiles, and you can determine the pattern for the URLs for those tiles, and configure your Gaia GPS account to display those tiles.

Most Common Method

The most common way to import a map source is to use this web form on gaiagps.com . It guides you through locating the URL for the source, naming it, and then displaying tiles once it's working correctly.

Other Methods

You can also use external software such as MapBox, or create a small data file called a TileJSON file, to import map sources. See the articles here for more information on these methods.

Manage/Use Map Sources on gaiagps.com

On gaiagps.com, add and manage your map sources by going to your custom map sources list . You can also access that list by clicking the account menu (at the top right) and selecting "Map Sources."

Manage/Use Map Sources in the Gaia GPS App

After import, sync your device ( iOS / Android ).

The source will appear in the "Map Source Categories" menu under "Custom Imports."

Known Issues

Elevation Loss and Gain Discrepancies

When creating a route, the elevation loss and gain are reported differently when editing than after the route is saved. This is due to how the information for the path the route follows is processed after being saved.


Many users report that the elevation stats after saving are higher than expected by between 10-30%. You may find that the more accurate ascent and descent stat can be viewed in edit mode.

We're are continuing to monitor this issue and evaluating what the best potential fix might be.

Hike Search Descriptions: General Guidelines

Give back to the hiking community by sharing the places you love. This article contains some best-practice tips for writing helpful, concise descriptions for cities, parks and forests, and hikes within the Hike Search platform. Click below for more specific advice on each page category:

  • City Pages. (Example: Denver )

  • Park and Forest Pages. (Example: Mount Rainier National Park )

  • Hike Pages. (Example: Angels Landing )


Things to Keep in Mind

  • Be as specific and accurate as possible

    • If an area is known for its wildflowers, say what kind and what months they bloom.
    • If there are steep drop-offs to a river below, try to say how many feet it is to the bottom
    • If there are cliffs or rock formations, try to say what kind of rock it is.
    • If an area has interesting cultural significance, briefly describe that culture and how you might encounter it on a hike.

  • Double check any facts against reliable sources.

    • Websites of official land managers (NPS, BLM, or USFS, etc)
    • local guides, national or state park rangers, or experts
    • scientific journal articles or government or university websites
    • guidebooks
    • Blogs are a great starting point for information but their facts should be double-checked from reliable sources.

  • Never copy/paste from any other sources, including NPS or government websites.

    • First off, it's plagiarism.
    • Secondly, we're trying to create original descriptions aggregated from both local knowledge and reliable sources in a new and more organized way.



  • Focus descriptions specifically around hiking .

    • Similar pedestrian sports like backpacking, trail running, or snowshoeing are OK as well.



  • Try to only write about trails you have explored extensively.

    • Don't guess about descriptions you're unsure about — pick a different place to write about, or ask an expert.



  • Use Markdown to style your descriptions.

    • Bold or italicize text and add links. Here's a cheat sheet .

How to Edit Segments

  • Start by reviewing official web pages to make sure your knowledge is current.

    • This could be the government-administrated NPS.gov or USFS.gov site, or a city or state's official tourism organization website.

  • Click “Edit” under the description you want to edit.

    • This will open up the text boxes for each segment.

  • Write in the Edit Segment box.

    • Describe why the park or recreation area is great for hiking. Be concise and accurate. (See General Guidelines.)



  • Double check facts.

    • Can't remember something, or can't find it online? Call up the rangers or park personnel.

  • Add links.

    • Any time you want to provide logistical information that could change, use Markdown to provide readers a link. For example, link to NPS web pages that list things like:
      • parking and permit information
      • public transportation schedules
      • pet policies
      • current road closure info
      • weather updates

  • Hit Save.

    • This will update the page with your changes.

Write a Hike Description

Hike pages will tell readers how difficult the hike is, what the scenery is like, an any major points of interest hikers might pass along the way.

First, read the General Guidelines for writing descriptions. Then, check out the tips below to decide what to include in each segment.

Overview

Give the reader a short description of what makes this hike great. Include:

  • Whether it's strenuous
  • Whether it's crowded (and, if so, how to avoid the crowds)
  • What the scenery's like
  • One or two specifics about the biggest draws of the place

Getting Started

Include the logistics of getting to the place.

  • How to get to the trailhead
  • Where to park
  • Any permits or parking fees required to hike
  • Any permits or fees required to camp/overnight for a backpacking loop
  • Any environmental concerns (like restoration areas or delicate plants that would require staying on-trail)
  • Points of interest not to miss

Taking Children

Describe whether this is a good option for family hikes.

  • Any unexpected scrambling?
  • Poisonous plants or other dangers?
  • Strenuous terrain?


Example

Write a Park or Forest Description

A description for a park (or forest or other natural area) will tell readers where the area is, what it's known for, and what kinds of scenery and terrain they can expect on hikes there.

First, read the General Guidelines for writing descriptions. Then, check out the tips below to decide what to include in each segment.

Overview

In a few sentences, tell us what makes this park (or forest, etc.) special.

  • Focus specifically on what stands out about the area and contributes to the best hiking experiences.

Getting Started

These are the essential details. This will likely be the longest, most in-depth section.

  • Do the parking lots get jammed?
  • Are there ways to avoid the congestion, like shuttles or bus systems?
  • Are there awesome places to visit off the typical tourist's itinerary?
  • Bathrooms and potable water available to hikers?
  • Any “don't miss” areas and specific hikes?
    • Link back to Gaia GPS pages on those hikes or points of interest when you can.

When to Go

Focus on when to go to enjoy the best hiking and similar sports.

  • Include the best (or worst) time to visit.
  • Consider school breaks and holidays.
  • Mention whether your park is famous for seasonal phenomena (fall colors, bird migrations, wildflowers, ice formations, etc)

Permits and Entrance Fees

Are there special permitting restrictions? Entrance fees?

  • NOTE: Do not list costs as they may change. Instead, link to permit and entrance fee information pages.

Dog Info

Some parks prohibit dogs or have special regulations.

  • Are pets are allowed?
  • Do they need to be on a leash or off?
  • Include a link to more complex regulations.

Example

Write a City Description

City pages tend to be more general than hike pages, but providing more specific details will give readers a better idea of whether this is a place they want to visit. These pages will tell you where the area is, what it's known for, and what kinds of things you could expect to see on a hike here.

First, read the General Guidelines for writing descriptions. Then, check out the tips below to decide what to include in each segment.

Overview

Give the reader a short description of what makes this city great for hiking. Include:

  • Activities/sports the area is known for
  • What the scenery's like
  • One or two specifics about the biggest draws of the place
  • Avoid writing about non-hiking related tourism, like local restaurants or urban sightseeing.
